## The Myth

A widely shared travel tip suggests removing prescription labels from medication bottles before international flights. The reasoning: customs officers won't ask questions if they can't identify what you're carrying.

**This advice is dangerously wrong.**

## What Actually Happens

In most countries, carrying prescription medication without proof of prescription is a criminal offense. Customs officers in countries like Japan, Singapore, and the UAE don't skip medications they can't identify — they flag them for inspection.

Without a label, you cannot prove:

- The medication was prescribed to you
- The quantity is for personal use
- The substance is legal in the destination country

## Real Consequences

Travelers have been detained at airports, had medications confiscated, and in serious cases faced criminal charges for carrying unlabeled controlled substances. Japan's Stimulants Control Act, for example, treats unlabeled amphetamine-class medications as contraband regardless of whether you have a prescription in your home country.

## What You Should Do Instead

1. **Keep medications in their original pharmacy containers** with the prescription label intact
2. **Carry a copy of your prescription** or a letter from your doctor
3. **Check the legal status** of your medication in your destination country before you travel
4. **Apply for permits in advance** if your medication is restricted — some countries require 14–30 days lead time
